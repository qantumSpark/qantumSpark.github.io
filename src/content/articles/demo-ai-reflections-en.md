## The Illusion of Foreknowledge

There is a persistent temptation in both artificial intelligence research and broader scientific culture to equate computational power with predictive capability. The assumption runs roughly as follows: given enough data, enough parameters, and enough compute, a sufficiently advanced model should be able to predict the future state of any system. This assumption is not merely optimistic -- it is, in important respects, mathematically false.

Chaotic systems, by definition, exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions. No amount of model sophistication can overcome the fundamental limits imposed by measurement precision in such systems. Weather prediction, turbulent fluid dynamics, and certain classes of economic behavior all share this property. A language model trained on historical weather data might produce plausible-sounding forecasts, but plausibility is not accuracy. The map, once again, is not the territory.

## Complexity Beyond Chaos

Yet chaos is only one source of unpredictability. Genuinely complex systems -- those involving adaptive agents, feedback loops, and emergent social dynamics -- introduce a deeper form of irreducibility. The behavior of a financial market is not merely chaotic; it is reflexive. The predictions made about the market become inputs to the market, altering the very dynamics being predicted. This is a form of computational irreducibility that no external model can fully resolve, because the model would need to simulate itself as part of the system it models.

Large language models, despite their impressive pattern-matching capabilities, remain fundamentally interpolative. They identify statistical regularities in past data and project them forward. When the future resembles the past, this works remarkably well. When it does not -- at precisely the moments when prediction matters most -- these models fail in ways that are difficult to anticipate, because failure at the boundary of the training distribution is, by definition, outside the model's experience.

## What Remains Valuable

None of this diminishes the practical utility of predictive models. Approximation, when understood as approximation, is enormously useful. The danger lies in mistaking the model's confidence for the world's predictability -- a confusion that grows more tempting as models become more fluent in expressing their outputs. The task ahead is not to build models that predict everything, but to build cultures of use that understand where prediction ends and judgment must begin.
